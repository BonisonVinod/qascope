# Marketing Playbook

How to get warm leads at the door without spending money. Written for the same stage as `SALES.md`: pre-paying-customer, founder-led.

> Marketing at this stage isn't about brand awareness. It's about manufacturing **warm intros** in volume so the sales playbook has fuel. Until you have 5+ paying customers, every marketing dollar is wasted; every marketing *hour* spent on warm-intro generation is gold.

---

## Brand & positioning (one paragraph)

Write this once and use it everywhere.

**Working version:**

> QAScope is the AI QA copilot for Indian SMB BPOs. Upload conversations, get every one scored against your rubric, with a coaching note per agent and a manager dashboard, in under 15 minutes. Built by an ex-BPO ops person who got tired of QA teams reviewing 5% of calls and missing 95% of risk.

Three details that matter:

1. **"Indian SMB BPOs"** — narrows the audience, attracts the right buyer, repels the wrong one
2. **"15 minutes"** — quantified onboarding speed; rare in the B2B world
3. **"Built by an ex-BPO ops person"** — credibility from operator-fit, not from technology

This is your bio, your pitch deck cover, your LinkedIn headline, your email signature. Don't deviate until you have data telling you to.

## Channels — by priority

### 1. Warm intros (highest priority, lowest cost)

**Where 90% of pipeline should come from until you hit 10 customers.**

**Sources to mine:**

- Your former Ops Director (and his network)
- The OM/QM friends who tested QAScope (their colleagues, vendor lists, ex-colleagues)
- Anyone you've worked with in BPOs, even briefly
- Industry events you attended in your previous role
- Vendors that sell *to* BPOs (telecom resellers, CRM consultants) — they have wide rolodexes and love being introducers

**The ask, verbatim:**

> "Hey [name], I've built an AI QA tool for SMB BPOs — already have a couple of pilots running. Could you point me to 1–2 ops or QA managers in your network who might find this interesting? I'm not selling, just looking for honest feedback. Happy to do a 30-min walkthrough on their data."

**Why this works:** the introducer is doing a 60-second favor, not a multi-hour pitch. Make it easy for them to say yes.

**Track it:** every intro request goes in a spreadsheet. After 1 week, no response = polite nudge. After 2 weeks, no response = move on. Don't fight asymmetry of attention.

### 2. LinkedIn (medium priority)

LinkedIn is the only social channel that matters for B2B India.

**What to post (in this order, weekly cadence):**

1. **Operator stories.** "When I ran ops at [previous role], here's the kind of compliance fail QA teams missed because they only reviewed 5% of calls." Real, specific, no AI mention. Builds the "you're one of us" rapport.
2. **Case study snippets.** Once you have one paying customer, post one specific outcome per week with their permission: "After 4 weeks, [customer] went from 8% to 94% conversation coverage. Here's what changed in their coaching cycle." (Anonymize if needed.)
3. **Behind-the-build posts.** "Today I rebuilt the column-mapping for CSV uploads because three different BPOs sent me CSVs with three different formats. Lesson: never assume your customer's data shape." Shows you ship; attracts other operators.
4. **Industry observations.** "QA in India is broken because [specific thing]." Polarizing but factual; generates comments.

**What NOT to post:**

- Generic AI hype ("AI is changing everything!")
- Sales pitches in disguise
- Corporate fluff without specifics
- Quote-sharing without your own take

**Cadence:** 2 posts per week, max. Quality over volume. Engage in comments — that's where actual prospects identify themselves.

### 3. Industry communities (slow burn)

- **NASSCOM events** — even attend the small ones. The hallway conversations are the value.
- **Customer Experience Conferences in India** — CX Summit, ICCS, Customer Service Week.
- **WhatsApp groups for BPO operators** — get added by your former colleagues. Lurk first, contribute occasionally, never sell.
- **Regional ones** — Chennai/Pune/Bangalore have local BPO operator groups.

**Goal at events:** 5 substantive conversations per day, not 50 business cards. Each substantive conversation = one row in your sales spreadsheet.

### 4. Cold outbound (lowest priority for now)

Don't do this until your warm pipeline is fully tapped. Cold conversion rates are 1–3%; warm is 30–50%. Spend hours where they pay.

When you do start cold:

- **Tool:** Apollo.io / Lusha for India BPO contacts. Use sparingly — quality > quantity.
- **Targeting:** "Director of Operations" or "Head of QA" at companies with 50–200 employees in BPO/customer support sector.
- **Email format:** 3 sentences. Specific reference to their company. One ask: *"Open to a 15-min call?"*
- **Volume:** 20 emails/day, hand-personalized. No mass blasts.

## Content backlog (low-friction items to produce)

These take a few hours each and create lasting marketing assets. Pick one per week.

| Asset | Audience | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Free QA rubric template** (Google Doc) | QA managers | Backlinks; downloads = leads |
| **"Hidden cost of 5% sampling" calculator** (web page) | Ops directors | Quantifies pain; bookmarkable |
| **YouTube: 5-min product walkthrough** | Skeptics | Sales tool; "watch this then book a call" |
| **Case study PDF** (1 page) | Procurement | First customer's story; shareable artifact |
| **Industry survey: "State of QA in Indian BPOs 2026"** | Industry-wide | Publishes data nobody else has; positions you as thought leader |
| **Podcast appearance** | Ops community | Free credibility; one episode = 6 months of warm leads |

## Website (don't over-invest)

Right now your "website" is `qascope-xyz.vercel.app`. That's fine for now.

**Minimum viable site (build in a weekend after you have 1 paying customer):**

1. **Landing page** — hero with the 1-paragraph positioning, 3 screenshot blocks (dashboard / review queue / report), pricing table, "Book a 15-min demo" CTA
2. **Pricing page** — same as `/billing` but public; honest about what's in each tier
3. **Privacy + terms** — boilerplate template, just enough to satisfy procurement
4. **Customer story** — once you have one paying customer, one 800-word post
5. **About / contact** — your name, photo, ex-BPO operator bio, email

**Don't build:**

- Blog (yet)
- Resources hub
- Webinar sign-ups
- Free trial wizard (your real "trial" is a 30-min call with you)
- Anything with chatbots

**Domain & branding:** if you don't have `qascope.com` / `qascope.app`, get one for ₹1,000/year. Use it for email (`bonison@qascope.app` looks professional) and the marketing site.

## Customer story (when you have one)

The first paying customer's story is the most valuable marketing asset you can produce. Treat it as a project.

**The story format:**

1. **Before** (1 paragraph): the problem, in their words. Specific numbers.
2. **The intervention** (1 paragraph): what they did with QAScope.
3. **After** (1 paragraph): outcomes, also specific numbers.
4. **What they'd tell others** (1 paragraph): direct quote.

**Where to use it:**

- 1-page PDF for sales calls
- Long-form LinkedIn post
- Embedded testimonial on website
- Email signature link
- Pinned tweet
- The first 60 seconds of every demo

**Permission:** ask for it explicitly. Frame as "we're a small startup, your story would help us reach others like you." Most early customers say yes if treated well. Offer them a discount in exchange.

## Lead magnets (for after you have 5+ customers)

When the LinkedIn audience starts engaging, give them something worth their email address.

**Strong options for QA-tool audience:**

1. **"The QA Manager's Coaching Template"** — a Google Doc with how to write coaching notes that actually change behavior
2. **Compliance checklist for [industry]** — banking / telecom / e-commerce versions
3. **Free 1-week QA audit** — they send you 20 conversations, you send back a manual review + AI scoring side-by-side
4. **Webinar: "How we cut QA review time 80% at [customer]"** — once you have a customer success story

**Don't do:** generic AI ebooks, "5 trends in AI" posts, anything that doesn't include specific BPO details.

## Metrics to track (weekly review, 15 min)

| Metric | Where | Healthy |
|---|---|---|
| Pipeline rows added this week | Spreadsheet | 3+ |
| Discovery calls booked | Spreadsheet | 1–2 |
| Pilots active | Spreadsheet | 2–4 (more = overload) |
| LinkedIn post engagement | LinkedIn | trending up |
| Warm intros received | Spreadsheet | 1+ per week |
| Closed-won this week | Spreadsheet | 0–1 (it's slow) |
| Closed-lost this week | Spreadsheet | track reasons |
| MRR | Razorpay / billing | gradually growing |

If MRR is flat for 4 consecutive weeks, **that's the signal to change something** — pricing, ICP, channel, or product. Don't double down on what's not working.

## What you should NOT do at this stage

- **Don't run paid ads.** Money is wasted before you know your ICP and conversion math.
- **Don't hire a marketing agency.** They sell volume; you need precision.
- **Don't write a 50-page playbook for hypothetical sales scenarios.** This document is enough; iterate from real calls.
- **Don't try to become an "AI thought leader."** Stay in your lane: BPO operations expert who happens to ship AI tools.
- **Don't go viral.** Viral attention = wrong audience = noise. Concentrated, targeted reach beats broad reach 10×.
- **Don't enter awards / "top startups" lists.** Vanity, no leads. (Exception: industry-specific BPO operator awards — those are real.)

## The honest summary

At your stage, **marketing is just structured warm-intro hunting**. Spend 80% of your marketing time generating intros and 20% on long-form content (LinkedIn posts, case studies). Anything else is procrastination dressed up as work.

Read this doc once. Then go ask your former Ops Director for two intros. That's the work.
